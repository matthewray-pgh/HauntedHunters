# OBSIDIAN FREQUENCY

### Production Script v1.0 — Incident Report / CapCut Pipeline

---

# Script Notes

## Runtime Target
7–8 minutes | ~750–900 words of narration

## Voice Direction
Same Archivist treatment as Videos 01 and 02 — ElevenLabs, tape recorder
playback, slight wow and flutter, subtle hiss, narrow frequency range.
This is the founding trauma of the entire Directorate. The Archivist
should sound most restrained here, not most dramatic — the weight of
the material should come from the facts, not the delivery.

## Visual Approach
Three visual systems in this video:

- **[DOCUMENT]** — the classified incident report. Same document
  treatment as Videos 01–02. Typewriter font, aged paper, CRT overlay,
  Ken Burns pan.
- **[TESTIMONY CARD]** — survivor testimony, presented as transcribed
  interview fragments. Black background, stamped TRANSCRIBED —
  SOURCE WITHHELD. Distinct from the Archivist annotation card
  (different stamp, different font treatment — these are someone
  else's words, not the Archivist's). **Silent. Text only. No
  narration ever plays under a testimony card.**
- **[LOCATION INSERT]** — the hand-drawn excavation map, inset into
  the document page. Same convention as Video 01's field documentation
  card.

**[FULL FRAME]** used once — maximum impact moment, per house style.

## On-Screen Text
No on-screen Archivist text during narration. Testimony cards are the
exception built into this video's format — they are always text-only,
always silent, always exactly as written below. Do not paraphrase them
in narration before or after they appear.

## Silence Note
[BEAT] — minimum two seconds.
[LONG BEAT] — minimum four seconds.
Testimony cards carry their own silence requirement — see each card's
notes. This video has more silence in it than either Video 01 or 02.
That is correct. Do not fill it.

## Image Transition Note
Document and location insert: slow dissolves, minimum four seconds hold.
Testimony cards: hard cut in, hold, hard cut out — no dissolve. A
testimony card is not part of the document; it interrupts it. The cut
should feel like the reader has been handed a separate piece of paper.
